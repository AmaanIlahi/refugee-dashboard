Problem & Context

What problem are you exploring?

We examine forced human displacement, focusing on global refugee and asylum patterns driven by conflict, persecution, and instability. Visualizations help simplify these complex global trends and make them easier to understand.

Why is this interesting?

Understanding where people flee from and where they go highlights humanitarian pressure points. It helps inform global policy, resource allocation, and long-term support systems for displaced populations.

Understanding the Context

More than 110 million people were forcibly displaced in 2023 — the highest number in recorded history. These movements reflect deep global instability, long-term conflicts, and sudden humanitarian emergencies.

Data Source

Data comes from the UNHCR Population Statistics Database and Kaggle, covering refugees, asylum seekers, internally displaced persons, and stateless populations worldwide.

Q1 – What is the current global landscape of forced displacement?

Key Insights

  • Darker regions on the map show high levels of displaced populations in 2023.
  • Middle East: Syria and Yemen remain major crisis zones with massive displacement.
  • Africa: Sudan, South Sudan, Ethiopia, and D.R. Congo carry some of the world's largest burdens.
  • Latin America: Venezuela's crisis continues to drive large-scale migration.
  • Europe, North America, and East Asia appear lighter - indicating fewer displaced people living internally.
  • Displacement is highly uneven; a few crisis regions bear most of the global burden.
  • Highlights the need for strong global cooperation and support for frontline states.

Q2 – How has global displacement changed over time?

Key Insights

  • Global displacement rose from ~30 million (2000) to over 60 million (2023).
  • Internally displaced persons (IDPs) drive most of the growth, especially post-2010.
  • Refugee numbers increase sharply after crises in Syria, South Sudan, and Afghanistan.
  • Asylum seeker and stateless populations also show gradual long-term growth.
  • The steepest increase occurs after 2015 due to overlapping global crises.
  • Trend shows displacement is rising fast and requires long-term humanitarian planning.

Q3 – From which countries are people fleeing the most?

Key Insights

  • Syria is the largest source of refugees due to prolonged civil war.
  • Afghanistan remains a major origin because of decades of instability.
  • Many African countries - Somalia, South Sudan, Sudan, D.R. Congo - appear due to ongoing conflict.
  • Myanmar appears because of ethnic persecution and targeted violence.
  • Top refugee origins share themes of war, weak governance, and humanitarian crises.
  • Identifying origin countries helps direct aid and peacebuilding efforts.

Q4 – How have asylum destinations changed from 2000 to 2016?

Key Insights

  • Asylum destinations shift frequently - no country stays at the top consistently.
  • Germany rises sharply after 2010 due to the Syrian refugee crisis.
  • The U.S., France, U.K., and Sweden appear regularly because of strong asylum systems.
  • South Africa ranks high within Africa due to regional migration patterns.
  • Crisis-driven spikes cause countries like Italy, Hungary, and Turkey to rise temporarily.
  • Asylum flows reflect global conflict hotspots and strong migration corridors.

Q5 – How do asylum application patterns differ across regions?

Key Insights

  • Global asylum applications follow a seasonal cycle - lowest in winter, peak in late summer.
  • Europe shows the strongest winter drop due to harsh travel conditions.
  • During major crises, seasonal patterns disappear - people flee year-round.
  • 5-year and 3-year windows show transitions driven by wars, border policies, and migration routes.
  • Stable years show clean seasonal waves; crisis years flatten the pattern.
  • Seasonality matters only when global conditions are stable - conflict overrides all patterns.

Q6 – Which countries host the largest refugee and asylum populations?

Key Insights

  • Pakistan & Iran: largest long-term hosts due to decades of Afghan displacement.
  • U.S. & Germany: major high-income hosts with strong asylum systems.
  • African hosts: D.R. Congo, Sudan, Tanzania carry heavy burdens with limited resources.
  • Turkey emerges as the largest host in recent years due to Syrian arrivals.
  • Hosting patterns reflect geography, long-term crises, and national asylum capacity.
  • Burden is highly uneven - many frontline countries need greater international support.

Q7 – How does asylum-hosting responsibility shift over time?

Key Insights

  • Hosting responsibility shifts dramatically with global crises.
  • Early 2000s: U.S., U.K., and Canada lead asylum receipt.
  • 2014–2017: Germany becomes #1 during the Syrian crisis.
  • 2018–2021: Colombia rises due to Venezuelan displacement.
  • Top 10 countries consistently handle ~70% of asylum seekers.
  • Smaller states (Lebanon, Cyprus) carry disproportionate burdens when normalized by population.

Q8 – What are the age and gender demographics of displaced people?

Key Insights

  • Children (0–17) make up nearly half of all displaced people globally.
  • Adults form most of the remaining group; elderly populations are small but very vulnerable.
  • Gender totals are balanced, but women face higher protection risks and caregiving burdens.
  • Child displacement grows fastest during major crises (Syria, Venezuela, Ukraine).
  • Women have greater exposure to gender-based violence and exploitation.
  • Humanitarian support must be tailored to age- and gender-specific vulnerabilities.

Q9 – How might displacement trends evolve in the future?

Key Insights

  • Long-term data shows steady and accelerating growth in displacement.
  • Forecasts suggest totals may reach ~130 million by 2030.
  • High-impact scenarios predict even larger increases.
  • Key drivers: conflict escalation, climate disasters, population pressures.
  • Children and vulnerable groups will form a growing share of displaced populations.
  • Host countries must prepare for long-term, large-scale humanitarian demand.
